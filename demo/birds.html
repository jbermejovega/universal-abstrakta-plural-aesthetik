Species spotlight

Four birds worth knowing

A starting point for new birdwatchers — common, striking, and easy to confirm once you know what to listen and look for.

  • European Robin

    Erithacus rubecula

    Bold and territorial, with a clear, melancholy song heard even in winter when most other birds stay quiet.

    • Garden bird
    • Sings year-round

  • Common Kingfisher

    Alcedo atthis

    A flash of electric blue along still water. Watch for a low, fast flight line just above the surface, then a perfect dive.

    • Riverside
    • Fast diver

  • Tawny Owl

    Strix aluco

    Rarely seen, often heard. The familiar "twit-twoo" is actually two birds calling back and forth, not one.

    • Nocturnal
    • Woodland

  • Ruby-throated Hummingbird

    Archilochus colubris

    Beats its wings up to 50 times per second and is the only bird that can fly backwards with full control.

    • Migratory
    • Nectar feeder
Getting started

Five habits of a decent birdwatcher

None of this requires expensive gear. Most of it is just learning to slow down and pay attention.

  1. 1

    Listen before you look

    Most birds are heard long before they're seen. Learn five common calls before chasing silhouettes.

  2. 2

    Note size, shape, then color

    Color fades in bad light, but silhouette and proportions stay consistent. Start there.

  3. 3

    Watch the edges

    Tree lines, hedgerows, and shorelines concentrate more species than open fields or water.

  4. 4

    Go out at dawn

    Activity peaks in the first two hours of daylight, when birds feed after a long, fasting night.

  5. 5

    Keep a simple log

    Date, place, and species. A year from now the pattern of what visits when will surprise you.
