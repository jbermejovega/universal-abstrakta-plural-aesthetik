Real sport. Really exists.

Chase a wheel of cheese down a near-vertical hill. Try not to die.

Every spring, dozens of competitors hurl themselves down Cooper's Hill in Gloucestershire, England, chasing an 8-pound wheel of double Gloucester cheese that can hit 70 mph. Whoever crosses the finish line first — on their feet or otherwise — takes the cheese home.

    • So… people chase cheese down a cliff?

    Essentially, yes. Cooper's Hill Cheese-Rolling and Wake is held annually near Brockworth, Gloucestershire, on the late May bank holiday. A round of Double Gloucester cheese is released from the top of the hill, competitors take off a second later, and gravity does the rest. Most racers don't run so much as tumble, roll, and bounce their way to the bottom — the cheese almost never gets caught.

    Since 2010 the event has had no official organizer due to safety and crowd concerns, yet it happens every year anyway, run entirely by volunteers and tradition. That informality is part of the appeal: no qualifying times, no sponsorships, no velvet rope. You queue up, you go.

    The (very few) rules

    Cheese rolling has survived for two centuries with almost no formal rulebook. Here's the entirety of it.

    1

    First past the post wins

    Cross the bottom of the hill before anyone else, on two feet or several more uncontrolled tumbles — it still counts.

    2

    You don't have to catch the cheese

    Nobody catches a 70 mph cheese wheel. Winning the race wins you the cheese regardless.

    3

    Catchers break your fall, not your race

    A line of rugby-player volunteers waits at the bottom to physically stop runners from careening into the crowd.

    4

    No age minimum, no real entry process

    Anyone willing to take the risk can take their place at the top of the hill. Helmets and pads are optional, not required.

    The injury report (this is the dangerous part)

    This isn't a novelty 5K. People genuinely get hurt, which is exactly why no official body wants to insure the event anymore.

    🟪 20+ Injuries in a single year (2013)

    Sprains, fractures, and concussions from one race alone, per St John Ambulance volunteers on site.

    🃏 1-in-2 Hill gradient at its steepest

    Roughly twice as steep as a standard wheelchair access ramp limit — in the opposite direction, at speed.

    🚫 2010 Year official organizing stopped

    Liability and crowd-safety concerns ended formal sponsorship; the race continues unofficially regardless.

    A very short, very weird history

    Nobody agrees on how this started. Theories range from a pagan fertility rite to a way of asserting grazing rights on the hill. What we do know comes from these milestones.

    Pre-1826

    Earliest known mention

    Local records reference the custom without explaining its origin, suggesting it was already an established tradition by then.

    1941

    Wartime pause

    The race was suspended during the Second World War, one of the few interruptions in its long, informal history.

    1998-2003

    International attention

    Television coverage turns a local custom into a global curiosity, drawing competitors from outside the UK for the first time.

    2010

    Official races end

    Crowd numbers reaching an estimated 15,000 make insurance and safety management untenable for organizers, who step back entirely.

    Today

    The unofficial races continue

    Locals and volunteers keep the tradition alive every late May, cheese wheel and all, with no governing body required.

    Frequently asked, rarely believed

    Wait, is this actually a real, sanctioned sport? ▾

    It's real and it happens every year, but there's been no official organizing committee since 2010. It runs entirely on local tradition and volunteer goodwill — which is also why there's no global governing body, ranking system, or prize money.

    Has anyone ever actually caught the cheese? ▾

    Extremely rarely, and usually only because the cheese hit an obstacle and slowed down first. At full speed it's simply too fast to grab safely, which is exactly why catching it was never a requirement to win.

    What happens to the cheese if nobody catches it? ▾

    Volunteers (or the crowd's safety nets) eventually stop it, and it's handed to whoever crossed the finish line first — cheese condition notwithstanding.

    Are there other cheese-rolling events besides Cooper's Hill? ▾

    Yes — smaller, tamer cheese-rolling customs exist elsewhere in the UK and have even been adapted abroad as charity fun runs, usually on far less terrifying slopes.
