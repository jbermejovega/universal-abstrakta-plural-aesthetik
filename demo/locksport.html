A hobby hiding in plain sight

The quiet, click-by-click sport of picking locks you already own

Locksport is the hobby and competitive sport of defeating locks non-destructively — for the puzzle, the craft, and the surprisingly social community behind it. No crime involved. Just tension wrenches, picks, and patience.

What is locksport, exactly?

Locksport is the practice of picking, decoding, or otherwise bypassing locks as a recreational and competitive skill — distinct from lockpicking used in crime or even from a locksmith's trade. Practitioners only work on locks they own or have explicit permission to manipulate, and most never touch a lock that's actually securing something.

🔓 Single-Pin Picking (SPP)

The classic discipline: manipulating each pin individually with a hook pick and tension wrench until every pin sets at the shear line.

Beginner friendly

⚡ Speed Picking

Timed competitions where pickers race to open standardized locks, often under 30 seconds for common padlocks.

🧩 Decoding & Impressioning

Determining a key's exact cuts without picking — by feel, by photograph, or by filing a blank against the lock itself.

🛡️ Locksport Security Research

Hobbyists who reverse-engineer lock designs and publish findings so manufacturers can fix weaknesses before bad actors exploit them.

From locksmith guilds to open competitions

Lock manipulation is centuries old, but locksport as an organized hobby is a recent, distinctly community-driven phenomenon.

  1. 1853

    American locksmith Alfred Charles Hobbs picks the supposedly "unpickable" Bramah lock at London's Great Exhibition in under an hour, sparking public fascination with lock security.

  2. 1997

    The Open Organisation Of Lockpickers (TOOOL) forms in the Netherlands, establishing an ethical code that still anchors the hobby: never pick a lock you don't own or have permission to test.

  3. 2000s

    TOOOL chapters spread internationally and begin hosting public "lockpick villages" at security and hacker conferences, teaching beginners hands-on.

  4. 2010s

    Online communities and YouTube channels turn locksport into a global hobby, with standardized practice locks and informal speed-picking leaderboards.

  5. Today

    Locksport conventions run dedicated competitive brackets, manufacturers sponsor "pick-resistant" challenge locks, and the hobby is taught openly at maker spaces and conferences worldwide.

The ethics that hold the hobby together

Locksport communities work because everyone agrees to the same boundary: this is a skill practiced on your own property, never used to bypass security that isn't yours.

The locksport code, in five lines

  • Own it or have permission. Never pick a lock that's actively securing someone else's property.
  • Disclose responsibly. If you find a real flaw in a commercial lock, report it to the manufacturer before going public.
  • Teach openly. Most clubs run free beginner workshops — the hobby grows through teaching, not gatekeeping.
  • Check local law. Possession rules for picks vary widely by country and state; know yours before you carry a kit.
  • Credit your sources. Locksport built its knowledge base in public forums and conferences — cite where techniques came from.

Frequently asked questions

Is locksport legal?

Practicing on locks you own is legal almost everywhere. Carrying pick tools in public is regulated differently by jurisdiction — some places require you to demonstrate the tools are for a lock you own, others restrict possession without a locksmith license. Always check local statutes before carrying a kit outside your home.

Do I need to be good with my hands or mechanically inclined?

No prior mechanical skill is required. Most beginners open their first cutaway lock within an hour of instruction — the hobby rewards patience and a light touch far more than dexterity.

Can locksport make me better at home security?

Yes — understanding how locks fail teaches you what to actually look for when buying one. Many practitioners end up recommending higher-security pin configurations or supplementary hardware after seeing how quickly basic padlocks can be defeated.

Where can I find a local locksport community?

Look for a local TOOOL chapter, a "lockpick village" at the nearest hacker or security conference, or a maker space that hosts beginner nights. Many groups welcome newcomers with loaner tools, so you don't need to buy a kit before trying it once.
