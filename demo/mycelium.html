The Wood Wide Web

Beneath every forest floor runs a living network older than any cable ever laid — threads of fungus carrying water, warnings, and sugar between trees that have never met above ground.

The Thread Itself

One organism, one cell wide, kilometers long

A single strand of fungal tissue — a hypha — is thinner than a human hair. Millions of them braid together into mycelium, a mesh so fine it can slip between soil particles too small for any root. A mycelial network under one hectare of healthy forest can stretch for hundreds of kilometers if unspooled end to end.

Trade Routes

What actually moves through the network

Mycorrhizal fungi sheath plant roots and push hyphae far past where roots can reach. In exchange for sugars the fungus cannot make itself, it ships back water and scarce minerals — mainly phosphorus and nitrogen — pulled from soil pockets the plant could never touch alone.

Early Warning System

Trees gossip through fungal lines

When aphids attack one plant, neighboring plants connected by the same mycelial network start producing defensive chemicals before the pest ever reaches them. The fungus doesn't just transport resources — it carries signals, turning an entire stand of trees into something closer to a single distributed organism.

"It's not a forest of competitors. It's a forest of cooperators, wired together below the surface we walk on."
— paraphrasing decades of mycorrhizal research
A Short History of Noticing

How we found the network

1885

Frank coins "mycorrhiza"

German biologist Albert Bernhard Frank describes fungus-root partnerships in forest trees for the first time.

1960s–70s

Radioactive tracers reveal flow

Labeled carbon and phosphorus let researchers watch nutrients move between connected plants through shared fungi.

1997

"Wood Wide Web" goes mainstream

Suzanne Simard's field experiments showing carbon transfer between Douglas fir and paper birch reach the cover of Nature.

Today

Mapping the underground

Genetic sequencing now lets ecologists trace which individual trees are wired to which fungal individuals, hectare by hectare.

Field Notes

Small facts worth carrying around

The biggest organism on Earth is a fungus

A single Armillaria individual in Oregon's Malheur National Forest covers roughly 9.6 km² of mycelium — and may be thousands of years old.

Mushrooms are just the announcement

The cap-and-stem fruiting body you see above ground is a temporary spore-dispersal structure — a tiny fraction of the organism's mass.

Disturbed soil breaks the line

Tilling, compaction, or heavy fertilizer can sever mycelial connections that took years for a fungus to build between hosts.

Not all trees share equally

Some studies suggest fungi may charge a "toll," moving more resources toward partners that supply more carbon in return.
